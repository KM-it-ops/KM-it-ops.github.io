After Eight Years on Federally Regulated Ground, an Analyst Reports for the SOC

Security+ certified and summa cum laude, Mahmoud “Michael” Al Kurdi arrives with a crew chief’s discipline, public repositories for receipts, and a simple ethos: every claim maps to evidence.

By The Security Operations Desk Charlotte, N.C.

CHARLOTTE — Security work rarely announces itself. For eight years it looked like this: international flights turning on schedule, identities validated at the door, access tightly controlled, and every irregularity written down — because on a federally regulated ramp, the paperwork is the security. From 2015 to 2023, Mr. Al Kurdi served as an Aviation Security Operations Crew Chief for American Airlines in Charlotte, holding a security clearance with a CBP badge endorsement and working international flight operations under FAA, OSHA, IATA, and CBP requirements — with recurrent incident-response and safety-and-security training as a standing condition of the job.

He has since put the discipline on paper. In January 2025 he earned the CompTIA Security+ ce certification (SY0-701, valid through January 2028). In December 2025 he completed a B.S. in Information Technologies with a cybersecurity concentration at Southern New Hampshire University — summa cum laude, 3.96 GPA, inducted into the Sigma Psi chapter of Alpha Sigma Lambda — conferred January 1, 2026.

The receipts are public. AgentForge, a configuration framework for agentic AI coding assistants, ships on npm with six adapter targets and a test harness standing behind it. Its companion, the AgentForge ATT&CKLens Benchmark, asks a strictly defensive question — can AI coding agents build secure, uncertainty-aware, MITRE ATT&CK-aligned defensive tooling? — and answers with seven scored artifacts and a re-runnable harness. Mr. Al Kurdi is seeking full-time work as a SOC analyst, security operations analyst, cybersecurity analyst, incident-response analyst, junior detection engineer, or in security automation. He is based in Charlotte, N.C., and open to remote.

The Person Behind the Ledger

The Making of an Evidence-First Analyst

The road from the ramp to the security operations floor ran through federal regulation, a 3.96 GPA, and a habit of writing everything down.

By The Security Operations Desk

Ask what an aviation security operations crew chief actually does, and the answer sounds familiar to anyone who has worked a SOC shift: validate identities, control access to aircraft and secure areas, document incidents to a standard a federal auditor could act on, and keep operations moving while doing all of it. Mr. Al Kurdi did that work for American Airlines in Charlotte for eight years — under a security clearance with CBP badge endorsement, across international flight operations governed by FAA, OSHA, IATA, and CBP requirements, with recurrent incident-response and safety-and-security training required year after year.

The habits transfer. Alert triage rewards the same temperament as flight operations: calm under time pressure, suspicion of tidy explanations, and documentation a stranger could pick up cold. His project work runs on the same ethos — rubric-based scoring, evidence citation, confidence scoring, and an explicit “No Clear Mapping” verdict where the evidence runs out, rather than a guess dressed up as an answer.

The road between the ramp and the SOC ran through coursework and delivery routes: courier and logistics work for USPS in Kannapolis (2024–2025), then delivery for Fossa Logistics, an Amazon DSP in Charlotte (2025–present), while completing the cybersecurity degree summa cum laude. The pattern is consistent — high-accountability work, done thoroughly, documented properly.

He now seeks the room where those habits are the job description: the security operations center — in Charlotte or from it.

Open Source · Section B Lead

“One Spec, Many Agents”: AgentForge Ships Its Configuration Framework to npm

Six adapter targets, one source of truth — with round-trip tests, smoke tests, and a doctor CLI standing behind the claim.

MIT License · github.com/KM-it-ops/AgentForge

The problem is mundane and universal: every agentic coding assistant wants its own configuration dialect. AgentForge keeps one specification and writes it out to six targets — Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI, Cursor, Aider, and a generic adapter — published to npm as @kmitops/agentforge, version 0.3.1, under the MIT license.

The proof is the point. Behind the package stand adapter round-trip tests, install smoke tests, a doctor CLI for diagnosing broken setups, a readiness runbook, and a live demo anyone can open. It is the same standard the Ledger applies to its proprietor: don’t claim it, show it.
